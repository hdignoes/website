Project Overview

Carbon dioxide emissions are largely responsible for the ongoing climate crisis. FortisBC asked our group to investigate a pathway for reducing environmental impact without significantly curtailing natural gas output. The Sabatier reaction — a much-studied but rarely industrialized process — converts CO2 and H2 into methane:

CO2 + 4H2  →  CH4 + 2H2O   ΔH = −165 kJ/mol

The reaction achieves over 99% conversion at elevated pressure over a nickel catalyst. Our process was scaled to handle ~1,400 kg/hr of biogas from FortisBC's Abbotsford, BC facility, which already has anaerobic digesters feeding natural gas into the city grid — significantly reducing infrastructure costs compared to a greenfield installation.

Biogas first undergoes dehydration (triethylene glycol contactor), followed by desulfurization using activated carbon adsorption. The sweetened gas then passes through a PSA unit: the RNG fraction goes directly to the pipeline, while the CO2 stream proceeds to the Sabatier reactor. Hydrogen is generated on-site via PEM electrolysis — effectively green hydrogen, since BC electricity is >95% hydroelectric. The reactor outlet is post-treated to meet FortisBC product specifications before pipeline injection.

Break-Even Scenarios

The base case projects a cumulative loss of ~$77 M over 20 years of operation. However, three credible break-even pathways were identified:

  • 1 A biogas feed of >3,000 kg/hr — a 2.1× increase in current plant capacity.
  • 2 RNG selling price rising to $0.91/kg (current market: $0.66/kg).
  • 3 Upgrading by-product oxygen from the PEM electrolyzer to medical-grade, which commands a significantly higher price per unit volume.
