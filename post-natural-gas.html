Natural Gas in a Sustainable World

The case for methane as part of a transition to clean energy

Energy Sustainability Chemical Engineering

Make no mistake — natural gas is not "clean" energy

Methane combustion, like that of all hydrocarbons, releases carbon dioxide. When someone says that natural gas burns "clean", what they really mean is that it burns completely — for every molecule of methane burned, we get exactly one molecule of carbon dioxide and two molecules of water. But that doesn't mean it's good for the environment. Carbon dioxide from methane combustion makes up a significant fraction of the USA's CO2 emissions.

 Key fact
1 kg of CH4 has the same global warming effect as 84 kg of CO2. Methane leaks are therefore not a trivial concern.

But that doesn't automatically make natural gas bad. It is among the best-understood and most energy-dense fuels we have, and engineers have been working on maximizing combustion efficiency since the late 1700s. As the world shifts towards wind, solar, and hydropower, a key challenge for engineers and policymakers is how to store excess capacity and how to meet demand when production is low — at night, for instance, or during calm weather.

The Energy Storage Problem

The most common large-scale form of energy storage is pumped hydropower, where water is pumped into an elevated reservoir during periods of surplus electricity and released through turbines when demand rises. But this system has real limitations. Capital costs are massive — a large impoundment dam can run well over $5 billion — and ongoing maintenance of dams and spillways is costly and meticulous. Beyond cost, pumped hydro requires significant elevation differences, making it unsuitable for flat regions like Florida, Minnesota, or Illinois. Its round-trip efficiency of roughly 70% also dampens its attractiveness.

The other obvious candidate is batteries. While they are already used for grid stabilization and operating reserves, at roughly $150/kWh they remain too expensive for true grid-scale seasonal storage. Lithium and cobalt supply constraints, relatively low energy density, and degradation from phenomena like dendrite formation continue to limit battery lifetimes and performance. True grid reliance on battery storage is still at least a decade away.

Enter: The Sabatier Reaction

The Sabatier reaction converts CO2 and H2 into methane and water over a nickel catalyst at elevated pressure and moderate temperature:

CO2 + 4H2  →  CH4 + 2H2O    ΔH = −165 kJ/mol

At around 10 bar and 270–400°C, the reaction achieves near-complete conversion with no significant side reactions in a plug-flow reactor. The process has been studied extensively but rarely commercialized at scale — its most famous application is aboard the International Space Station, where it is used to recycle CO2 back into oxygen and water.

The key insight for energy storage is that the required hydrogen can come from electrolysis — easily integrated into a solar or wind farm. When renewable electricity is abundant and cheap (midday sun, windy nights), that energy is used to split water and produce hydrogen. The hydrogen is then reacted with captured CO2 to produce methane. You are, in effect, storing clean electrical energy as a carbon-neutral fuel.

Why Store Energy as Methane Rather Than Hydrogen?

Hydrogen is notoriously difficult to store and transport. It has a very low volumetric energy density at ambient conditions, requires cryogenic cooling or extreme pressurization for storage, and hydrogen embrittlement can degrade metal pipelines over time. Natural gas, by contrast, benefits from decades of infrastructure investment. In North America alone, there are hundreds of thousands of kilometres of natural gas pipelines, vast underground storage reservoirs, and LNG terminals. Millions of homes already use natural gas for heating.

With a Sabatier plant near a renewable energy source or industrial CO2 emitter, that existing network becomes a conduit for carbon-neutral energy. Surplus BC hydroelectricity or Alberta wind power could be stored as RNG and shipped via pipeline to wherever it is needed — at a fraction of the cost of new battery infrastructure or new dams.

Is It Truly Carbon-Neutral?

Not perfectly. When the methane is combusted, the CO2 that was originally captured is re-released into the atmosphere. The cycle is carbon-neutral only if the electricity used for electrolysis is itself zero-carbon and the CO2 feedstock comes from a biogenic or direct-air-capture source. With BC's grid, which is over 95% hydroelectric, the first condition is nearly met. Waste CO2 from anaerobic digesters (biogas plants) satisfies the second.

Ultimately, it is not a perfect long-term solution — we should be working toward carbon-negative processes. But for the immediate future, the Sabatier process offers a pragmatic bridge: high energy density, well-understood thermodynamics and materials, compatibility with existing infrastructure, and a total cycle efficiency of over 80%. I hope to see it gain traction within the next five years.
